Food and habits

Tough topic.I know you probably don’t agree. Me neither.I don’t even agree with myself, I’m not asking you to agree with my contradicting self. I want to tackle this “problem” in the most logic, clear and hopefully concise manner.But first we need to put…Definitions! Heroes that always save the day. A value is a fairly abstract concept.

  • the regard that something is held to deserve; the importance, worth, or usefulness of something.
  • principles or standards of behaviour; one’s judgement of what is important in life.

Here it is more about the second definition (thanks Oxford): One’s judgement of what is important in life. Some properties of a value:

  • arbitrary
  • personal
  • irrefutable

Even though values can change after a long and hard work on oneself, or sudden realizations, or special events in life, here we will consider them mostly static or otherwise specified. We need to compose with our present values and live on. Collisions of values (cognitive dissonance) can make our life hard and decision making difficult. The purpose of this draft is to reduce if not eliminate cognitive dissonance. While this is a first step, we also need solid reasons to do so. Living with the disconfort of cognitive dissonance is just about an everyday challenge. Quickie on the topic, 4 ways of coping with it. Here the scene is a person on a weight loss diet and this person eats a doughnut (which is not allowed in this diet)

  1. Change the behavior or the cognition (“I’ll eat no more of this doughnut.")
  2. Justify the behavior or the cognition, by changing the conflicting cognition (“I’m allowed to cheat my diet every once in a while.")
  3. Justify the behavior or the cognition by adding new behaviors or cognitions (“I’ll spend thirty extra minutes at the gymnasium to work off the doughnut.")
  4. Ignore or deny information that conflicts with existing beliefs (“This doughnut is not a high-sugar food.")

Our Subject

I will take the case of Mr A, who is a human being perfectly standard. A has received a standard education and is living a standard life. It does not matter specifically for our purpose.

A has the following values (among others of course, like love, beauty…):

  • freedom : V1
  • animals : V2
  • the planet (its “health) : V3
  • his classic diet (basically everything edible, just to make it easy, but you can take the classic European diet) : V4

Assuming that he likes animals for standard reasons: humans tend to like ‘life’ and ‘wildlife’ and animals are mostly “cute”. He probably already established a bond with a cat, a dog, or any other animal. We assume furthermore that A wants to stay healthy for as long as possible. (let’s say V5)

This should correspond to a vast majority of people (excluding the pet thing, but it’s just about giving him a bit of a story). The goal here is to see what is conflicting within A’s values, what can he do regarding this. A concrete example is given at the end (the only one I can decently talk about).

CASE V2:

Inside A’s mind, there is a hidden conflict. It can be incoherent to like eating meat and to like animals. How can this conflict be resolved ? In practice it is usually not, the idea is simply masked : see Quickie 4. In this case, eating meat or animal products ‘doesn’t hurt animals all that much’ and this places an argument using V2 fairly low ranked. Let’s take the to possible cases here. Either his love for meat/animal products is stronger than his love for animals. This is the case V4>V2 that we will discuss right after. Or his love for animals is stronger than his love for meat/animal products, which makes the case V4<V2. These cases will be discussed with the idea of developing what is naturally implied by it.

CASE V4>V2:

The cognitive dissonnance can be solved by : admitting he does not really like animals (compared to meat/animal products). At this moment it means that he can eat all he wants, he can wear everything, for which V4>V2 stays true. Regarding the animal suffering issue: this is okay. If he eats the classic european diet, aka the supermarket meat, milk, eggs then this makes him commit to these facts (among others):

  • I’m okay with continuously keeping some animals pregnant and either raising their child to be pregnant regularly as well, or raising them for veal or beef
  • I’m okay with having animals in cages to get meat or eggs out of them (yes you might buy eggs from free roaming farms, but that’s not what are doing the people who produce your cordon bleu or your ravioli). Note that in some countries, caging animals is forbidden. Still you may want to look into France’s intense production. 17 to 22 chichens by square meters. (yes, that’s about the size of your keyboard per chicken)
  • I’m okay with supporting the fishing of tons of fishes at once, and letting them be dehydrated, suffocated or straight up obliterated by the weight above them on the deck of the fishing boat
  • I’m okay with having bovines lined up to get food all day long and then be killed. Not before dehorning, castrating and branding them

CASE V4~V2 or V4<V2 (sometimes yes sometimes no):

In this case, he really likes animals buuuut he also really likes their meat. He is sort of comparably “in between” and it’s understandable. I mean, bacon dude. In this case he cannot commit to the aforementioned facts. Apart from the obvious choice of stopping altogether, he has another choice: changing his habits. He has to do this with in mind his values and most notably V1.

V1 : Freedom. Is it a value that only applies to humans? Probably not. This is a recurrent theme : we want the animals to be free as much as possible. Free to roam, free to eat, free to lay eggs, not so free to keep them though, free to run and fly. All this already implies a lot of things. The animals can no longer be in cages or in extreme densities nor lined up until death. So they live constrained lives but relatively free. Note: This should be a case by case analysis but it would be incredibly tedious and long. Yet, we need to adapt to the sensibility of everyone, for each animal and each treatment, life conditions, people will have different levels of tolerance. Which is normal and we have to accept this.

Maybe having some chickens running around is good, they are kinda unhappy you took those eggs but well, they can run and fly and go outside. When they die, they die, they were not really raised for meat anyway. Well, no, it’s too expensive to leave them alive. Fun facts time!

“What typically happens is they just gas them with CO2, and asphyxiate them. They are not raised for meat. They don’t taste the same. What Mr A has to say? Maybe he’s okay with it, after all, they had an okay life.

That’s the same for free range meat chickens, they are just killed earlier.

So, Mr A can alleviate his issue by being more conscious of what he is consuming. “Not torturing animals is great” thinks Mr A.

But just as we will see in the next chapter, Mr A has to adapt to fit his values.

Bottom line: Mr A adjusts his consumption to his sensibility. What repulses him too much: that’s no-no. What feels okay is… okay. This doesn’t stop him from eating as much meat or dairy as he wants, he just needs to be reassured that it has been produced in conformance to his sensibility. All is good. He chose the path Quickie 1 and 4. (1 for changing his ways then 4 by saying hey that’s okay in the end, now they don’t really suffer that much)

If he decides that reducing the cognitive dissonance is not enough, he feels like he needs to go full Q.1. path, then he stops for good, as best as he can. (Within the limits of all his other values. If he feels this is limiting too much his own freedom, or happiness, or social relationships, he may want to use path 2 for a bit, or to adjust how he feels about all this. No one said it’s easy!)

CASE V3:

In face with environmental challenges, it’s normal that Mr A feels bad about consuming beef or meat in general.

With emissions estimated at 7.1 gigatonnes CO2 -eq per annum, representing 14.5 percent of human-induced GHG emissions, the livestock sector plays an important role in climate change. Beef and cattle milk production account for the majority of emissions, respectively contributing 41 and 20 percent of the sector’s emissions. While pig meat and poultry meat and eggs contribute respectively 9 percent and 8 percent to the sector’s emissions. The strong projected growth of this production will result in higher emission shares and volumes over time. Feed production and processing, and enteric fermentation from ruminants are the two main sources of emissions, representing 45 and 39 percent of sector emissions, respectively. Manure storage and processing represent 10 percent. The remainder is attributable to the processing and transportation of animal products. Included in feed production, the expansion of pasture and feed crops into forests accounts for about 9 percent of the sector’s emissions. Cutting across categories, the consumption of fossil fuel along the sector supply chains accounts for about 20 percent of sector emissions.Yes.
